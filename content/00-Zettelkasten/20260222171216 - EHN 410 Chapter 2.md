- The notation $b|a$ denotes that $b$ divides $a$
- We use the Euclidean Algorithm to determine the greatest common divisor between 2 positive integers.
- We know that two integers are relatively prime if their only common positive integer factor is 1.
- An interesting property of $gcd$ is that if $c$ is the $gcd$ of a $a$ and $b$ then any divisor of $a$ ***AND*** $b$ is also a divisor of $c$.
- In general $$gcd(a,b)=gcd(|a|,|b|)$$
![[Pasted image 20260222172953.png|center]]

- The euclidean algorithm assumes that $a$ is larger than $b$ and it iteratively finds the GCD through a number of pretty expensive division processes.
- Two numbers are said to be congruent modulo $n$ if $$(a~mod~n)=(b~mod~n)$$
- Fermat's Little Theorem is pretty cool and allows us to quickly calculate modulo for very large numbers. It says that if we are trying to find the modulo $p$ of a prime number then we can exploit this relationship $$a^{p-1}=1(mod~p)$$
- An alternative form of *Fermat's Little Theorem* that I don't like very much is described $$a^p=a(mod~p)$$
- Euler's Totient Function was discussed briefly but the function of it for any particular process I am not yet sure.
- Funnily enough, Euler's Theorem is presented next and it states that for every $a$ and $n$ that are relatively prime, $$a^{\phi(n)}=1(mod~n)$$
- Alternatively, $$a^{\phi(n)+1}=a(mod~n)$$
- The Miller Rabin Algorithm is used to test large number for Primality.
![[Pasted image 20260222175611.png|center]]
- The algorithm has 6 steps that we need to understand for a pretty simple implementation. Once it has found two values $k$, $q$ such that $$(n-1)=2^kq$$It then runs an iterative test on the primality.
- Before Miller Rabin Algorithm, an algorithm known as *AKS* was developed but it was not as efficient. The problem at the time was that the algorithms to prove primality in the past were all probabalistic.
- Chinese Remainder Theorem allows a way to manipulate quite large numbers mod $M$ in terms of tuples of smaller numbers. ![[Pasted image 20260222181453.png|center]] Yes it was Sun Tzu who developed the theorem.
