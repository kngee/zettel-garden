- The simplest method to implement a $DAC$ is to pull samples from memory and convert them into an impulse train.
- When combining the impulse train with a *zeroth-order hold*. To smooth this signal, we send it through a lowpass filter at $f_{max}$,.
- However, in the high frequencies you will get distortion because of the $sinc$ function. To avoid this, apply the inverse sinc function up to $f_{max}$.
- Even when using the built in $DAC$, it will implement the sample and hold but not the reconstruction filter so we will need to do that.

- Remember that the phase angle is measured with the positive x-axis.
- Decomposition and Synthesizing are both proceesses we can perform on a Discrete Fourier Transform.
- DC is considered offset.
- Remember that in the fourier analysis, the components other than the DC component are just multiples of the fundamental frequency.
- Decomposition takes the result and generates the components and the reverse is syntehsis.
- Sinusoidal input guarantees a sinusoidal output
- The discrete Fourier Transform is evolved from the Continuous Fourier Transform, we are correlating the time signal with all sinusoidal components at a certain frequency.
- For the discrete fourier transform, the integration become a summation and the $t$ is replaced by $n$.

- We refer to the results of the DFT is refered to as its spectral components.
- The DFT equation can be represented in either exponential or rectangular form.
- Understand that there is a difference between $n$ and $k$, where $n$ refers to the time domain index and $k$ refers to the index of the $DFT$ output.
- Inverse $DFT$ applies the scaling and note that the exponential term becomes positive.
- Note the Omega $\Omega$ term that we use in the $DFT$ equation.
- The choice of $N$ is very important as it determines the number of input samples needed, the resolution of the frequency-domain results and the amount of processing time necessart to calculate an $N$ point $DFT$.
- The $DFT$ is mirrored around $f_{max}$, the real components will be an even function and the imaginary components will be an odd function.

- The fundamential frequency of the sinusoids is $$f_{fundamental}=\frac{f_s}{N}$$
- When you oversample, this changes the fundamental frequency that you are going to sample with so be careful.
- Analysis frequences are the frequencies where we will have components, $$f_{\text{analysis}}(m)=\frac{mf_s}{N}$$
